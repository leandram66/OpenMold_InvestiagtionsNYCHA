Decrease in Open Mold Investigations for NYCHA Tenants

NYCHA residents and leaders of Manhattan Together and South Bronx Churches expose the ongoing challenges in relation to mold as open work orders start to see a decline in 2023.

Leandra Manon

Nov. 9th, 2023
Photo of NYCHA tenant wearing respirator. Taken by Ana Brigida

The Bronx Documentary Center hosted an open reception for the “Speak Out: NYCHA Tenants Fighting To Breathe Better At Home” exhibition, addressing mold & leaks inflated by NYCHA’s long list of backlog orders.

The exhibit features tenants coping with leaky ceilings with trash using trash bags and kids wearing respiratory masks to mitigate asthma attacks caused by the mold.

Former Manhattan Together and South Bronx Churches leader, Michael Stanley, marked the event as both a commemoration and celebration, reflecting on the 10-year anniversary of the Baez vs NYCHA case which sought relief for NYCHA residents affected by mold.

“This event is a commemoration of, to some extent…10 years since we reached the Baez vs. NYCHA consent decree on mold and leaks,” says Stanley.

“To another extent, it's more like a celebration of twenty, or by some counts, thirty years of organizing in public housing, and particularly around mold and leaks,” continues Stanley.

Despite the 2019 Ombudsperson Call Center’s introduction, improvements in mold-related issues only began this year.

“I think the important thing about this exhibit to know is that while even on mold and leaks, there are still far too many tenants who suffer needlessly, a system has been built up where some tenants are actually getting the repairs they need. Maybe 10 or 15 percent only, but that's a thousand times better than it was 10 or 15 years ago, and it's an example of if organized people stick with it long enough, it can, a real difference can be made,” expresses Stanley.
